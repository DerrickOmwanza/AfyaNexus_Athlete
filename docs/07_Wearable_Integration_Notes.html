1. Device Specifications

The T70 smartwatch is the baseline wearable currently represented in AfyaNexus. The integration layer is being evolved into a modular architecture so that other devices or health ecosystems can also provide athlete data automatically. This reduces reliance on manual athlete input and improves the accuracy of the injury risk workflow.

2. Data Captured by T70

The wearable and health-integration layer captures the following data points that are directly relevant to AfyaNexus:

Data FieldDescriptionAfyaNexus Usage
Heart Rate (Average) Average beats per minute during activity and rest Stored in WearableData table; used as ML input feature
Sleep Duration Total hours of sleep detected by the device Cross-referenced with manual sleep input in RecoveryLogs
Sleep Quality Deep sleep vs light sleep breakdown (where available) Supplementary recovery metric for ML model
Step Count Total steps taken during the day Activity level indicator stored in WearableData
Activity Logs Detected activity sessions (walking, running, etc.) Cross-referenced with manual training logs
Nutrition Intake Logs Calories, carbohydrates, proteins, fats, hydration and meal notes imported from connected nutrition apps where available Stored in NutritionLogs and reviewed by the nutritionist for performance support
Clinical Biomarkers (Future) Lab-derived nutrient or mineral measurements such as calcium, iron, or vitamin D Not collected by standard consumer wearables; future integration path via clinician upload or EMR/lab systems

3. Integration Flow

AfyaNexus uses a hybrid sync process. Training and recovery data can come from Bluetooth wearables or platform APIs, while nutrition intake can come from athlete entry or connected health and diet apps.

1
Athlete Connects a Data Source

The athlete pairs a wearable device or authorizes a supported health platform. For the first Android demonstration flow, AfyaNexus uses Google Health Connect as the synchronized source.

2
Athlete Triggers Sync

From the athlete dashboard, the athlete triggers a sync. The system connects through Bluetooth, mobile health bridges, or vendor APIs depending on the source. On Android, the preferred demonstration path is Health Connect.

3
Data Retrieval and Parsing

The system reads the available training, recovery, and nutrition-intake data. Raw source data is normalized into the AfyaNexus format.

4
API Submission and Validation

Parsed data is submitted to the sync endpoints with the athlete's JWT token for authentication. The backend validates the data, stores it, and flags suspicious or ambiguous values. Android Health Connect imports can combine recovery, wearable, training, and nutrition data into one sync request.

5
Dashboard Update

The athlete dashboard refreshes to display newly synced values alongside manual inputs. The injury prediction service can then recalculate the athlete's current risk score using the freshest dataset.

6. Limitations and Considerations

⚠️ Device-Specific Integration: T70 is the current baseline, but Garmin, Apple Health, Fitbit, WHOOP, and Android health ecosystems will require separate adapters, credentials, and approval flows.

⚠️ Bluetooth Range: Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) has a typical range of 10 meters. Athletes must be within range of their paired device during sync.

⚠️ Data Accuracy: Wearable and app-derived data are estimates. AfyaNexus should validate, cross-check, and flag conflicts between manual and synced entries before using them in prediction.

ℹ️ Manual Override: Athletes can still enter data manually. Manual data should remain available as a fallback and should be compared with synced data for ambiguity detection.

ℹ️ Android Bridge: AfyaNexus now includes an Android-first bridge concept in which a lightweight native app can request Health Connect permissions, read records locally on the phone, and send normalized payloads to the AfyaNexus API.

ℹ️ Future Expansion: Standard consumer wearables do not directly measure body nutrient levels such as calcium or protein status. Those metrics should be handled as a future clinical integration through laboratory systems, clinician upload, or EMR interoperability.
